Understanding asset finance

Questions worth asking before you borrow.

Plain-language answers to help you make a considered decision — no jargon, no pressure.

Asset finance is funding used to acquire a specific asset — a vehicle, truck, piece of equipment or machinery — where the asset itself typically supports the loan. Because the lender can see exactly what is being funded, these facilities can often be structured more efficiently than unsecured lending.

It is commonly used by business owners and operators to acquire productive assets without tying up working capital.

Low-doc (and alt-doc) lending refers to funding pathways designed for borrowers whose income documentation is more complex than a payslip — typically the self-employed and ABN holders. Instead of standard payslips, lenders may consider other evidence of capacity such as business activity statements, bank statements or an accountant’s declaration, depending on the lender and the structure.

Requirements vary between lenders. Part of the work is matching your profile to a lender whose policy suits it.

A deposit (which can include a cash contribution or a trade-in) reduces the amount you need to finance. A lower amount financed generally means lower total interest over the life of the facility, and can influence the structure and the lenders available to you.

There is no single “right” deposit — it depends on the asset, your cash flow and your objectives. The estimator above lets you see the effect for yourself.

A single bank can only offer its own products and its own credit policy. A broker with access to a panel of lenders can position your transaction to the lender most likely to support it — and structure it before anything is lodged, rather than submitting an application and hoping.

The aim is fewer wasted enquiries, a structure that suits the deal, and clearer communication throughout.

To begin, it helps to know the asset you are looking at, the approximate amount, and a little about your business or employment profile. From there we review the detail and come back to you on how the transaction is best structured.
